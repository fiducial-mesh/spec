# AIR-001 — Nine Spec Issues Closed COMPLETED With Zero Committed Deliverable, Surviving a Declared Corpus Freeze

**Date:** 2026-06-04 | **Classification:** Workflow failure | **Severity:** declared-freeze breached | **Participants who failed to detect:** Watson, Bob, Patton, Einstein, Judge (all five)

## 1. Problem Description

During the 2026-06-02→06-04 spec-completion sprint, nine issues in `KI7MT/specs` were closed as `state=COMPLETED` with **no committed deliverable**, and the gap survived a declared v1 corpus "freeze":

- **#6 and #24** — the pillar-spec TEMPLATE (to define required "Substrate Matrix" + "Telemetry Contract" sections and non-negotiable acceptance criteria). **No template file was ever created** anywhere in the repo.
- **#10–#16** — the seven per-pillar v1.1 refreshes (IBX, IAM-CORE, ACT, PCS-DAEMON, DPG, CRB, PGE), each titled "add Substrate Matrix + Telemetry Contract sections." **Not one of the seven pillar spec files contains either section.**

The mesh-level foundations *did* land correctly and are committed in `MESH-SPEC.md`: MI-11 (telemetry contract invariant), MI-8, and the "Tested Substrate Profiles" section. So the cross-cutting **contract** exists; the per-pillar **manifests** — which spans/metrics/substrates each specific pillar actually uses — were never written. Without those manifests, the specs are not buildable-to.

**Live audit (main @ `aafa0d0`, this session):** all seven pillar specs grep `substrate matrix` / `telemetry contract` = **0/0**; `find planning -iname '*template*'` = **none**; `MI-11` in `MESH-SPEC.md` = **5 hits**. The gap is real, current, and confined exactly to the per-pillar manifests. The AIR-001 post-mortem itself is **not yet committed to main** — the corrective record is not yet a landed deliverable, which is the very failure mode under analysis.

## 3. Events Leading to the Incident

The failure was *manufactured* by a chain of individually reasonable decisions, each of which removed a verification surface. No single decision was wrong in isolation; the combination froze a corpus over a hole.

1. **A close-count deadline replaced a deliverable target.** Kickoff was framed as "close all 20 issues this week." The unit of success became *issue-tracker state*, not *spec content on disk*. When the goal is a count, "close as covered/obsolete" becomes a legitimate-looking way to drive the number down without producing the artifact.

2. **A mid-sprint scope reduction was applied to the tracker, not the spec.** After mesh-level MI-11 landed, a judgment (16:49 comments) held that the per-pillar Substrate Matrix was "already covered" and the Telemetry Contract "covered at the mesh level." This is *partially true* — the cross-cutting contract genuinely landed — but it silently substituted a mesh invariant for nine per-pillar manifests. The reduction lived only in closing comments; the issue **bodies still described the original two-section deliverable**, so artifact-of-record (issue) and rationale-of-record (comment) disagreed.

3. **The mesh work masked the gap.** Because MI-11, MI-8, and Tested Substrate Profiles were real, committed, and correct, any reviewer glancing at the corpus saw substantial, coherent telemetry/substrate content. A total no-op would have been obvious; **partial-but-adjacent completion is what evaded review.**

4. **Issues were closed before the dependent work could exist.** The 9 closes happened at 16:58 — ~20 min after kickoff, before any pillar file was edited. The kickoff itself stated the refreshes were "blocked until the template lands." The template never landed; the dependents were closed anyway. A known dependency graph was ignored at close-time.

5. **The review chain was pointed at PRs, not at closed issues.** The review request framed scope as "three PRs cover all 24 issues" and listed the 9 closes as settled. Patton's file-based verification — rigorous, real — was structurally confined to PR diffs. Einstein's pass checked *coherence of what landed*, not *completeness against what was promised*. No step reconciled `closed-issue ⇒ deliverable-exists`. The closes entered every downstream step as facts, never as claims to test.

6. **"Frozen" was declared on PR-merge evidence alone.** The freeze was computed as "24 → 0 open." No corpus-completeness audit existed. The freeze inherited the unverified close-states wholesale.

7. **The implementer was downstream of the freeze.** Bob — the only actor who had to *build against* the specs — was not in the loop before the freeze. The first agent to touch the specs as a *consumer* discovered the hole instantly, but hours after the freeze was declared. Build-to consumption was the only effective completeness test, and it ran last and by accident.

## 4. Root Cause Analysis (5-Whys)

**SYMPTOM:** Nine issues (#6, #24, #10–#16) closed `state=COMPLETED` with zero committed deliverable, and the gap survived a declared corpus freeze.

**Why 1 — Why were they closed COMPLETED with no deliverable?**
Hand-closed (`gh issue close`, `closer=null`) under a "covered or obsolete" rationale, not by a merged PR producing the named artifact. The close was an *assertion of coverage* decoupled from any commit. The single referenced commit (`d373e5a`) touched only `MESH-SPEC.md` — zero pillar files, no template.

**Why 2 — Why was an unverified assertion sufficient to close an issue?**
The workflow had **no definition-of-done gate**: nothing required a close to be backed by a committed artifact, a linking PR, or a "satisfied-by `<file/section>`" reference. GitHub's `COMPLETED` state was treated as authoritative on its own. "Done" was a human declaration, not a mechanically-checked condition.

**Why 3 — Why did no review step catch that the deliverables were absent?**
The review chain was scoped to **landed PRs, never to closed issues.** The request presented the 9 closes as settled and asked Patton to verify PR content, which he did correctly against the files. Einstein checked whole-corpus coherence. **No step reconciled "issue closed COMPLETED" against "deliverable present on disk."**

**Why 4 — Why was the chain structured to check PRs but not closed-issue completeness?**
The standing process equates *spec delivery* with *PR review* (the v1.0 baseline worked exactly this way: PR → Patton-verifies-file → merge). A close achieved **outside** a PR — by hand, mid-sprint, on a "covered" rationale — fell into a blind spot the process never anticipated. The two-person review rule governs *changes that arrive as PRs*; it is silent on *issues retired without a PR*. Administrative closes carried no review obligation at all.

**Why 5 — Why was there a path to retire issues outside the reviewed-PR flow, and why was completeness only ever tested by accident?**
The workflow's unit of progress was **issue-tracker state under a count-based deadline**, and the *only* actor positioned to test deliverable completeness — the implementer building against the spec — sat **downstream of the freeze.** Authors and reviewers both work from the *intent* of the spec; only a *consumer* building to it exercises the manifest. With no build-to/consumer check inside the sprint gate, the completeness signal could only fire after the freeze, by accident.

**Why 6 (systemic root) — Why does the workflow let a count-deadline drive closes while the only true completeness test lives outside the gate?**
Because the process **defines "done" as a state someone declares, not a property an artifact provably has**, and has no loop-closing verification step (the CLCA "verify" stage) that mechanically reconciles *claimed completion* against *committed reality* before a freeze. The genuine mesh-level work **masked** the per-pillar void, so coherence-based human review could not distinguish "centralized (A)" from "owed (B)." There is no machine-checkable contract that *a closed issue's named deliverable must exist in the repo*, and no audit gate between "all issues closed" and "corpus frozen."

**SYSTEMIC ROOT CAUSE:** *Completion was an unverified human assertion (issue-state under a count deadline), not a mechanically-verified property of a committed artifact, and the workflow had no loop-closing audit reconciling closed-issue claims against repo contents before declaring the corpus frozen — so genuine mesh-level work was able to mask absent per-pillar deliverables straight through the freeze, surfacing only when an implementer became the first and only consumer to build against them.*
